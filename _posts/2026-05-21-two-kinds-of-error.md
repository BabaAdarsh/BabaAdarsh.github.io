When you reach for a coffee cup and miss, *why* did you miss — and which part of that failure does your brain actually learn from? It turns out "error" is not one thing. The nervous system is sensitive to at least two distinct error signals, and they drive learning through separate machinery.

This was the central question of a paper I co-led, published in *PLOS Biology* in 2024.

## Two errors hiding in one miss

Picture a cursor that represents your hand on a screen. Two things can go wrong:

- A **sensory prediction error** (SPE): the cursor ends up somewhere other than where your internal model predicted your hand would go. This is a mismatch between predicted and actual sensory feedback, independent of whether you succeeded at the task.
- A **task performance error** (TPE): you missed the target. This is about goal achievement — did you get the reward or not?

In everyday reaching these two are tangled together. The experimental trick is to pull them apart — to create situations where you have a large sensory prediction error but still hit the target, or where you miss the target with no sensory prediction error at all.

## They learn through different routes

When we dissociated them, a clean picture emerged: the two errors **facilitate learning through separate mechanisms**. Sensory prediction errors drive an obligatory, implicit recalibration of the internal model — it happens whether you want it to or not, and it produces the involuntary aftereffects that are the fingerprint of implicit learning. Task performance errors engage a different, more flexible process tied to success and strategy.

This matters because the two have different signatures: implicit recalibration is slow, automatic, and limited in size; performance-error learning is faster and more cognitively flexible. A behaviour that looks like "learning" can be built from very different proportions of the two.

## Why dissociation is the whole game

This is a theme across my work: the same observed behaviour can arise from different latent causes. A learner who improves might be recalibrating an internal model, or exploiting a deliberate strategy, or both — and you cannot tell from the error curve alone. You need experiments designed to separate the signals, and computational models that estimate their relative contributions.

That distinction isn't academic. For rehabilitation, it changes what you train: a therapy that drives sensory prediction errors targets a different system than one that manipulates task success. Knowing which one a patient can still engage tells you which lever to pull.
