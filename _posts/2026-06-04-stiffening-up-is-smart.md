Tense, uncertain, or under pressure, we stiffen. A novice on skates locks every muscle; a surgeon steadies a hand against tremor. This co-contraction — firing a muscle and its opponent at the same time — looks wasteful. Why fight yourself? It turns out to be one of the nervous system's cleverest control strategies, and it sits at the centre of my postdoctoral work.

## Impedance you can dial

When agonist and antagonist contract together, the joint they span gets stiffer. The limb resists perturbation more strongly and returns to its intended path faster — without waiting for the relatively slow loop of sensing an error and correcting it. The brain is, in effect, turning up the mechanical gain of the limb in advance.

That's valuable precisely when the world is unpredictable. If you don't know which way you'll be pushed, raising stiffness everywhere is a reasonable hedge. The cost is metabolic — co-contraction burns energy and does no net mechanical work — so the system should deploy it selectively, only when the expected benefit justifies the cost.

## The body is part of the inference

Here's the twist that interests me most. Stiffening doesn't only change how you *move*; it may change how well you *sense*. Proprioception — your sense of limb position — depends on signals from muscle spindles, and those signals are modulated by the very co-contraction that stabilizes the joint. So the act of controlling the body can reshape the information the brain receives *about* the body.

That makes the body an active participant in inference, not merely the output end of a motor command. Bodily state — muscle activation, co-contraction, impedance, coordination — feeds back into the estimates the brain uses to predict and control. This is the theme of one of my research umbrellas at Queen's: embodied sensing and neuromuscular control.

## Measuring it

We get at this with multichannel surface EMG and motor-unit recordings while people perform reaching, stabilization, and perturbation tasks — manipulating cognitive load, uncertainty, and mechanical demand. The questions: does uncertainty drive co-contraction up as a protective strategy? Does that change sensory precision? Do individual differences in muscle-synergy structure predict who adapts well and who doesn't?

These are tractable, low-cost human experiments — and they make excellent training projects for students learning biosignal analysis, signal processing, and computational modelling on real data.

So co-contraction is not a failure of relaxation. It's the brain buying stability and reshaping its own senses, on demand, at a price it's willing to pay. Stiffening up, used wisely, is smart.
