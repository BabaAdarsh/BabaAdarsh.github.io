# Research

Humans rarely act in stable environments. We learn multiple behaviours across changing contexts, rely on uncertain sensory feedback, and must constantly infer which past experience is relevant to the present moment. My research asks how the nervous system solves this problem — how prior experience, contextual information, bodily state, and sensory evidence are integrated to predict, learn, and control behaviour.

I use motor behaviour as a tractable model system because it permits precise experimental control over feedback, uncertainty, context, and trial-by-trial adaptation. Movement is not a narrow endpoint here, but an experimentally rigorous window into general principles of prediction, inference, learning, and behavioural flexibility.

## Behavioural prediction and learning systems

A central challenge in cognitive neuroscience is understanding how the brain selects which prior experience should guide current behaviour. Poor performance may not reflect a failure to learn — it may reflect expression of the *wrong memory*, an inappropriate mixture of memories, or an inflexible learning rule.

My work shows that behavioural flexibility depends on two separable processes: inferred context determines **which memory is expressed**, while error structure, feedback timing, and task history determine **how the behavioural policy is updated**. Recent work demonstrates that memory expression is systematically biased by learned transition statistics (Kumar et al., *iScience*, in press), that model-based and model-free mechanisms leave durable imprints on subsequent learning (*J Neurophysiol*, 2025), and that distinct error signals drive learning through separate mechanisms (*PLOS Biology*, 2024).

## Embodied sensing and neuromuscular control

Prediction and learning depend not only on external context but on internal estimates of the body. Proprioception, muscle activation, co-contraction, and coordination patterns make the body an active part of inference — not merely the output of a motor command.

At Queen's, I investigate how agonist-antagonist co-contraction shapes action, proprioceptive perception, and motor-unit function, using multichannel EMG, motor-unit recordings, adaptive psychophysics, and KINARM exoskeleton tasks.

## Neural mechanisms and neurorehabilitation

As part of the multi-university **CanStim** program on brain stimulation for stroke neurorehabilitation, I analyze local field potentials and multi-electrode array recordings to examine how visual and proprioceptive inputs are integrated across primary motor and premotor cortices during goal-directed reaching — and how these network dynamics are disrupted after focal ischemic lesions in a non-human primate stroke model. The long-term goal: interpretable, mechanism-grounded targets and biomarkers for neurorehabilitation.
